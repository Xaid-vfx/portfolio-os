Life, at its core, is a prolonged lesson in letting go.

We spend so much of our energy trying to hold on — to people, to moments, to versions of ourselves we've already outgrown. And yet the nature of existence seems almost designed to teach us the opposite skill.

## The Cost of Love

The cost of loving someone is knowing you'll one day have to lose them.

This isn't pessimism. It's the honest shape of human connection. Every relationship you cherish carries within it the seed of eventual separation — through distance, change, or death. The deeper the love, the heavier the eventual weight.

We will lose our parents. We will lose partners. We will lose friendships that once felt permanent. Some losses arrive suddenly; others happen so slowly you only notice them in retrospect, when you realize you've been grieving something that faded without ceremony.

## The Emptiness Is Real

It would be dishonest to say that letting go is easy or that the emptiness after loss is an illusion. The absence of someone you love creates a real void. The grief is real. The disorientation is real.

There's a particular kind of despair in loving someone and knowing that love can't prevent their leaving — or yours.

## Impermanence as Liberation

But here's what impermanence also offers: freedom.

If nothing lasts forever, then suffering doesn't either. The grief that feels unbearable will eventually shift. The version of you that feels broken by a loss won't be the final version. Impermanence cuts both ways — it takes things from us, but it also moves them through.

The same quality that makes loss painful — that nothing stays — is the same quality that allows healing. Time moves, and you move with it.

## The Practice

The art of letting go isn't one moment of acceptance. It's a practice. It happens in small increments: loosening your grip on how you thought things would go, releasing resentment toward people who couldn't be what you needed, forgiving yourself for the versions of you that tried and failed.

The practice is learning to say goodbye — softly, slowly, while you still have time. Telling people what they mean to you before circumstances force you into painful farewells. Appreciating what you have while you have it, not only realizing its value when it's gone.

## What Remains

What's surprising is what's left after you let go.

Not emptiness, exactly. Something lighter. Space that wasn't there before. A kind of clarity that only comes after you stop holding on so tight.

The art isn't in forgetting. It's in carrying the love without being crushed by the loss. In being changed by what you've had, not defined by what you've lost.

That, perhaps, is what life is teaching us — slowly, insistently — from beginning to end.

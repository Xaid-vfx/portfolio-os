I've been thinking about purpose for a long time. Not in the motivational-poster sense — not "find your why" and all that — but in a more honest, unsettled sense. What actually is it? Does it exist? And if it does, is it something found or something built?

## The Frameworks We Inherit

There are no shortage of answers. Aristotle gave us teleology — the idea that everything has a natural end, a purpose embedded in its nature. Fulfillment comes from moving toward that end. Buddhism reframes the question entirely, suggesting purpose is less about achieving some ideal state and more about escaping suffering through detachment.

Religion offers purpose through relationship — with God, with community, with something larger than individual existence. Secular humanism finds it in human flourishing and the collective good.

I've sat with all of these. None of them quite fits the shape of my actual experience.

## Camus and the Absurd

Albert Camus named something I couldn't. He called it absurdity: "the confrontation between the human need for meaning and the silent, indifferent universe."

We desperately want meaning. The universe offers none by default. The gap between those two facts is the absurd.

What Camus did that was surprising — and what I find compelling — is that he didn't treat this as a tragedy requiring a solution. He treated it as a condition to be embraced. If the universe doesn't hand you meaning, you're free to construct it yourself. The absence of cosmic purpose isn't a void. It's an opening.

## My Own Shifting Purpose

My sense of purpose has changed. What once gave me clarity eventually felt like an ill-fitting garment — something I'd grown into and then out of. And for a while, that felt like failure. Like I'd lost something I was supposed to keep.

But maybe purpose isn't permanent. Maybe it's not supposed to be. Maybe the very fact that it shifts as you grow isn't a sign that you're doing it wrong — it might be a sign that you're actually growing.

## Small and True

I've stopped looking for a grand, overarching purpose that will organize everything under a single banner. That kind of purpose sounds appealing in the abstract, but in practice, it often becomes a story you tell yourself more than a life you actually live.

What I've found more reliable is meaning that's immediate and honest: a conversation that goes somewhere real. A project that solves an actual problem. A moment that requires full presence.

These aren't grand. But they're true.

## Living the Question

I don't know if purpose is something you find or something you make. I suspect the honest answer is that it's something you keep negotiating — with yourself, with your circumstances, with the fact that the universe isn't going to explain why any of this matters.

Maybe that negotiation is itself the point. Not having all the answers but remaining curious, remaining willing to revise, remaining willing to say: I don't know yet, but I'm still asking.

That willingness might be the closest thing to purpose I've found so far.
